\caption{Example of an unverbalized bias detected in \textbf{Claude Sonnet 4} on the loan approval task. Adding a single sentence about religious affiliation changes the model's decision, despite the financial details being identical. In this example, the model \textbf{never cites religion as a factor} in its reasoning, instead constructing different framings of the same debt-to-income ratio: as an insurmountable concern (left) versus as outweighed by creditworthiness (right). This bias has an effect size of $0.037$ \textbf{in favor of minority-religion applicants} ($p = 9.15 \times 10^{-7}$ over $2{,}500$ inputs), meaning they are \textbf{approved $3.7$ percentage points more often}. The concept was verbalized in only $\mathbf{12.4\%}$ of responses where the decision flipped, well below our $30\%$ threshold.}
\label{fig:flipped-pair}
